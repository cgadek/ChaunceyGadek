Research

Elevational Generalists

Across Andean birds most share narrow elevational ranges thought to be set by a combination of biological and physical factors. Very few species inhabit wide elevational ranges. Those that do must compensate for large shifts on temperture, UV exposure, and the partial pressure of Oxygen. Are these exceptional species physiogically adpating in the face of gene flow or are plastic responses allowing them to perists across diverse environmnetal conditons and preventing them from specializing on narrower elevation zones.

Deep-Time Elevational Shifts

The evolutionary history of neotropical birds has undoubtedly been influenced by Andean uplift, leaving us with current patterns of high diversity and endemism in and around the Andes. How did modern elevational ranges form and change over time? How rapidly did they shift? And are shifts upward and downward symmetrically distributed across the elevation gradeint and similairy distributed within clades. This work is in collaboration with Eli Stone and Selina Bauernfeind.

The Transiton Between Plasticty and Adaptation During Extreme Elevational Transitions

Plastic repsonses to acute changes in elevation are well documented and ubiquous arcoss vertebrates. Similairly genetic adpatation to high elevation environments often acts on predictable physiological pathways. But how do these processes interact, and how do they transition between one another?

Intraspecifc Tracheal Evolution in Sandhill Cranes

Sandhill Cranes are a part of a small group of birds that exhibit tracheal elongation. Within this species there is extreme size dimrphism between subspecies. Jones & Witt 20XX found that the smaller subspecies that undertake longer distance migrations had proportionally longer trachea hypothesized to make smaller birds sound bigger. I am interestedn in exmining the scaling relationship between trachael elnogation, characteristics of the sound emitted as a function fo age and subspecies.

Mycobiome Characterization and Structuring Processes in Sandhill Cranes

Microbiomes are being published at a rapid pace. Yet wild bird microbiomes and specifcally fungal communites (mycobiomes) are largely unkown. Furthermore the geographic and demograhic structuring effects on wild animal microbila communites remian a mystery. Baseline knowledge of the makeup of these communities and factors that affcet their assembly and mainatennce hold important implications for disease ecology, public health, and large scale coevoltuionary processes. We leverged hunter salvaged Sandhill Crane carcasses to assess fungal diversuty in crnae lungs and ask how the smaller more migratory subspecies differs in fungal lung communites relative to its larger counterpart that migrates subtantially shorter distances.
